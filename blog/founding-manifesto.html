Founding Manifesto · 9 June 2026

We Couldn't Find the Village. So We're Building One.

A few years ago, a group of us started having the same conversation across different cities and different time zones. We wanted to live differently — not in an abstract, someday-we-should way.

At dinner tables, over messages that went on too long, on evening walks where we said things we usually didn't: that we wanted to live differently. Not in an abstract, someday-we-should way. In a where-do-we-actually-want-to-wake-up-in-ten-years way.

We wanted to know our neighbours. We wanted land we could tend. We wanted our children — or the children around us — to grow up with dirt under their fingernails and a real sense of where food comes from. We wanted evenings that felt unhurried, and a community where helping each other wasn't a transaction.

We weren't romanticizing it. We'd all lived in cities long enough to know what we were giving up and what we weren't. We just couldn't shake the feeling that the life most of us were living — optimized, efficient, largely indoors — was missing something structural. Not a holiday. Not a wellness retreat. Something we could actually build a life inside.

We looked. We kept walking away.

The options we found were real, and some of them were beautiful. Eco-villages in rural Europe with decades of history. Co-housing projects in Scandinavian cities. Land cooperatives in the UK. Intentional communities of every shape and philosophy.

But they all had something that stopped us. Most required you to move in full-time to a place already decided — the land, the culture, the people, the rules — fixed before you arrived. Some were inspiring from a distance but felt closed on the inside; communities that had evolved their own gravity over years, where joining meant fitting in rather than helping shape something. Others were organized around a shared ideology so specific that you had to agree with it entirely before you could even apply.

And the ones that weren't like that — the straightforward land-share projects, the rural co-ops — often had no professional governance, no clear ownership structure, and no path to resolving the conflicts that inevitably came up when people shared land and money and proximity.

There wasn't a model that said: here is a well-governed, legally sound, genuinely co-owned piece of land, with real nature on it, built around regenerative values, where the community shapes its own culture from the start. Where you own a real stake — not a membership fee, not a time-share, not a donation — but actual equity in actual land.

We couldn't find it. So we started building it.

What Regenerative Village is — and what it is not.

Regenerative Village is a co-ownership model for shared land. The people who join us will co-own the land together, governed by a transparent structure that protects individual co-owners and the commons as a whole. We are searching for the right piece of land now — somewhere with good soil, clean water, proximity to a real town, and enough space for the community to breathe.

When we say regenerative, we mean it specifically. Practices that restore soil, water, and biodiversity over time — not as an aesthetic, but as the point. Permaculture, food forests, managed grazing if the land calls for it. We want to leave the land in better condition than we found it, and that goal shapes every decision we make.

It is not a commune. You will have your own space — your own home — with privacy and real boundaries. You will not be required to share your income, your politics, your spiritual practice, or your time beyond what you freely choose. There is no guru. There is no ideology you need to sign.

It is not a holiday resort or a wellness retreat. We are building something people will actually live in — or live part of their lives in — not visit for a weekend.

It is not a speculative investment. It may appreciate over time. It may not. If you are joining for financial returns, this is the wrong place.

It is not finished. We are at the beginning — searching for land, building the founding community, designing the governance structure together. If you join now, you are not buying into something complete. You are helping build it from the ground up.

Who this is for.

We are looking for people who are serious about this — not necessarily ready to move tomorrow, but genuinely thinking about how they want to live over the next decade or two. People who care about land, about community, about doing things properly. People who have the capacity to be co-owners: financially, emotionally, practically.

We are not looking for a uniform type. Families and individuals. People in their thirties and people in their sixties. City people who want more time in nature. People who already live rurally and want more community. People who have tried other alternatives and found them lacking.

What we are looking for, above everything else, is good faith. People who will show up when it matters, hold disagreements without breaking things, and take care of shared resources as if they own them — because they do.

Why now.

We are not the first people to want this. Intentional communities have existed for as long as people have been dissatisfied with the ones they were born into. What feels different now is the combination: land getting harder to access individually, a growing number of people who have spent years in cities and want something else, and a moment of climate and ecological urgency that makes regenerative land use feel genuinely important — not merely pleasant.

We are also building this with proper governance infrastructure from the start. The legal structures, the ownership models, the decision-making frameworks that have caused previous projects to fracture. We are doing this carefully. We are taking our time on the land search, because the wrong land at the wrong price would compromise everything that follows.

An invitation.

We are not asking you to buy anything yet. We are not asking you to commit to anything. We are asking you to join our waitlist so that when we are ready — when the land is found, the governance is tested, the ownership structure is clear — you are in the conversation.

If this is something you have been thinking about for years, we want to hear from you. If you have questions, doubts, or hard-earned scepticism from watching other projects fail, we want to hear that too.

The people who build Regenerative Village with us will not be customers. They will be co-owners. That changes everything about how this works — and about what we can actually become.

The village is not built yet. But it is being built.
